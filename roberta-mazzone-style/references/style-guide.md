## Local Reference Set Observations

The user's screenshots show a coherent slow-travel editorial look:

- Mostly vertical or near-vertical crops.
- Repeated use of windows, doors, arches, balconies, and shaded alleys as natural frames.
- Sea and architecture appear together often: terraces, open windows, coastlines, cliff beaches, and seaside rooms.
- People are present but rarely frontal portraits: back views, profiles, silhouettes, cropped hands, hats, linen garments.
- Everyday objects become anchors: coffee, books, chess/backgammon, straw hats, hammock, dining table, shutters.
- Palette is warm and tactile: cream, linen white, olive/ivy green, terracotta, honey sun, muted cyan-blue sea, dark espresso shadows.
- Light is usually real and directional: late afternoon sun, strong window shafts, golden side light, shadow geometry.

## Style DNA

The feeling should be: "a quiet, sunlit Italian day remembered later." It is elegant, but not over-polished; aspirational, but grounded in small rituals.

Core traits:

- Architecture as composition, not background.
- Travel as atmosphere, not checklist.
- People as scale and mood, not performance.
- Natural materials and tactile details.
- Warm highlights with substantial, soft-edged shadows.
- Muted color and restrained contrast, with occasional deep blacks.
- Mediterranean leisure: sea, terraces, books, coffee, linen, shade.

## Scene Families

### Coastal Terrace / Sea View

Use when the image has sea, balcony, window, hammock, chair, table, or cliff.

- Frame through a window, doorway, railing, pergola, or shaded terrace.
- Keep the horizon calm and simple.
- Let one object carry the mood: open book, chair, glass, hat, hammock, plate, or curtain.
- Grade sea as dusty blue/cyan, not tropical neon.
- Preserve warm walls and deep shade.

### Italian Alley / Doorway

Use when there are old streets, arches, shutters, stone, plaster, or facades.

- Compose vertically with a strong central passage or side-lit wall.
- Use shadow as a graphic mass.
- Let the distant sea, tree, window, or lamp be the visual reward.
- Keep color earthy: ochre, terracotta, faded plaster, deep green shutters.

### Sunlit Interior / Still Life

Use when the image has a room, table, bed, bathroom, game board, coffee, book, or window.

- Use hard sunlight and diagonal shadows.
- Crop close enough that objects feel touched by a person.
- Keep whites creamy and textured, not sterile.
- Let dark corners remain dark.
- Use grain and gentle softness rather than clinical sharpness.

### Architecture And Garden

Use when facades, ivy, doors, old houses, stairs, or courtyards dominate.

- Let the building face the camera squarely if symmetry is strong.
- Place a small person, hat, or garment as a quiet scale cue.
- Keep foliage rich but not neon; preserve leaf texture and shadow.
- Let aged surfaces show patina.

### Fashion / Figure In Place

Use when a person is central.

- Prefer back view, side profile, or partial body crop.
- Style with linen, straw hat, knitwear, neutral dress, simple sandals.
- Avoid hard fashion posing; use walking, leaning, looking, reading, pouring coffee, or pausing.
- Prioritize relation to place: figure should belong to the architecture or landscape.

### Mountain / Snow Travel

Use when snow or mountains appear.

- Keep warmth in clothing, skin, or late sun.
- Use muted blue sky and cream snow rather than icy cyan.
- Let the person be slightly soft or secondary if the mountain is the emotional anchor.

## Composition Rules

- Prefer vertical crops: 4:5, 3:4, 2:3, or Instagram-story vertical.
- Use 1-2 strong lines: doorway edge, window frame, shadow edge, horizon, wall, or table stripe.
- Keep one dominant anchor and one secondary detail.
- Leave breathing room around the subject.
- Crop hands/objects intentionally; partial views feel natural.
- Let foreground shade frame a bright exterior.
- Use centered symmetry only when architecture supports it.
- Avoid busy tourist scenes; wait for quiet gestures and clean shapes.

## Light Rules

- Favor late afternoon, golden hour, or sun entering from a side window.
- Do not flatten shadows; this style needs shadow mass.
- Slightly underexpose bright white walls, linen, and sea sparkle to protect texture.
- Use backlight or side light for hats, hair, curtains, bottles, cups, and water.
- Let interiors sit warmer than exterior sea/sky.

## Color Grade

Base palette:

- Cream / linen white: warm, slightly yellow, textured.
- Sea blue: dusty cyan or muted teal-blue.
- Green: olive, ivy, cypress, sage; reduce neon yellow-green.
- Earth: terracotta, ochre, sandstone, walnut, espresso.
- Black: deep but not crushed everywhere.

Editing moves:

1. Warm highlights slightly.
2. Cool or neutralize blue sky/sea only enough to keep them calm.
3. Pull green saturation down, shift yellow-green toward olive.
4. Keep skin and linen natural.
5. Add gentle S-curve; avoid harsh HDR.
6. Add subtle grain and a small amount of halation only around strong sun.
7. Use local masks to darken corners and emphasize window/door frames.

## Shooting Guidance

- Lens: 28-50mm equivalent for everyday scenes; 24-35mm for interiors and architecture; 70mm+ for compressed sea/mountain details.
- Aperture: f/2-f/4 for object/person intimacy; f/5.6-f/8 for facades and architecture.
- Exposure: protect highlights on white walls, linen, and sea glitter.
- Timing: late afternoon, early morning, blue-to-gold transition, or strong midday sun if shade geometry is interesting.
- Direction: side light and backlight are stronger than flat front light.

## Critique Checklist

Ask:

- Is the place emotionally legible?
- Does architecture organize the frame?
- Is the light real and directional?
- Is there one quiet human or object anchor?
- Are whites warm and textured?
- Are greens and blues muted enough?
- Do shadows add mood instead of hiding mistakes?
- Is the image elegant without looking staged?

## Common Corrections

- If the image feels too ordinary, add a frame-within-frame: window, doorway, arch, curtain, railing, table edge.
- If it feels too cold, warm highlights and shift neutrals toward cream.
- If it feels too busy, crop tighter and keep one object/gesture.
- If the greenery feels digital, desaturate and shift toward olive.
- If the sea feels postcard-bright, darken and mute cyan/blue.
- If the subject feels posed, use a back view, profile, or hand-detail crop.
- If the edit feels modern and glossy, reduce clarity, add grain, and preserve imperfect texture.
